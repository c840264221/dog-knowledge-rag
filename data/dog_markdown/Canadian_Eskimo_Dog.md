## 📖 关于该犬种
The Canadian Eskimo Dog is a pre-Columbian breed. The Thule people, the forebears of today¿s Inuit, started using dogs as draft animals between 1,000 and 1,250 years ago. Northern Alaska is the original homeland of the CED. From there the Thule people spread east to the Canadian Artic and to Greenland between 800 and 1,000 years ago, with their dogs. CEDs and Greenland dogs are closely related.The Canadian Eskimo Dog is an aboriginal breed. Its 1100- to 2000-year history is closely intertwined with that of the Thule culture of Inuit, who, following the Dorset culture, occupied the coastal and archipelago area of what is now Arctic Canada. The existing strain of Canadian Eskimo Dog originated from stock primarily bred by the Eskimo Dog Research Foundation in the Northwest Territories. The foundation¿s work over a six-year period was primarily funded by the Governments of Canada and the Northwest Territories and involved the purchase of specimens from the remnant population of dogs kept by the Inuit of the Boothia Peninsula, Melville Peninsula and parts of Baffin Island. The Canadian Eskimo Dog, as a primitive dog, is primarily a carnivorous breed, whose natural diet consisted of seal, walrus, fish or caribou. For centuries this breed was used as a draught animal and could pull between 99 and 176 lb. per dog, covering distances from 15 to 70 mi per day. He was also used as a hunting dog, to locate seal breathing holes for the Inuit hunters. As a hunting dog he would also attack and hold at bay musk ox and polar bear for the Inuit hunters. In the summer the dog was used as a pack dog carrying up to 33 lbs.

## 🧾 历史
The Thule, the ancestors of today¿s Inuit, were the first people to start using dogs as draft animals, hunting aids, and guardians (primarily against polar bears). This is how the Canadian Eskimo Dog came into being. CEDs originated in Alaska, the homeland of Inuit culture. It was from there that the Thule spread east to the Canadian Artic and Greenland between 800 and 1,000 years ago, with their dogs. CEDs thrived in the Canadian Artic until a series of events that started in the 1950s caused the breed to become endangered. The first one was a policy that the Canadian government pursued of forced relocation of Inuit people from seasonal camps to government-created permanent settlements. When the relocation occurred, the RCMP (Royal Canadian Mounted Police) shot dogs that hampered the relocation effort. The second event was a devastating distemper outbreak in the early 1960s and diseases such as rabies and canine hepatitis, which decimating thousands of Inuit Dogs in Canada¿s Eastern Artic. The third event was the introduction of snowmobiles, which destroyed the Inuit's partnership with their dogs. The Canadian Artic was once home to an estimated 20,000 CEDs. Between 1960 and 1970, there were reportedly only a few hundred left. In 1972 a revival of the Inuit Dog got underway thanks to the efforts of the Eskimo Dog Research Foundation in the Northwest Territories. The existing strain of CEDs originated from stock bred by that foundation.

## 🩺 养护指南
### Exercise
CEDs have an innate instinct to pull. Activities like dog sledding, bikejoring, skijoring, backpacking, and canicrossing are ideal. Other activities like weight-pulling competitions and playing with other dogs in a fenced-in yard are also recommended.

### Training
The Inuit people train their dogs to pull from the time they are puppies, albeit with very light objects. CEDs should not handle heavy loads until they are at least twelve-months-old.

### Nutrition
CEDs diet should include raw meat with ground bones and veggies and kibble especially made for Artic dogs that has a high fat content. The size of portions depends on the age of the dog and the number of meals a day (1 or 2).
