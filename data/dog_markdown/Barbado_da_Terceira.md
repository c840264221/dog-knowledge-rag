The Barbado da Terceira is a medium-sized herding dog from Terceira Island in the Azores, Portugal. Preservation efforts focus on functional structure, temperament, long-term health, genetic stewardship, and the instinctive working ability that defines the Barbado as a true farm breed.Intelligent and perceptive, the Barbado thrives with consistent training, meaningful work, and clear direction. It forms a strong bond with its owner and remains affectionate with its people while staying attentive to its surroundings. Playful and sometimes clownish, it pairs charm with serious working ability. Its rustic, beautiful appearance and structure reflect Portugal¿s agricultural heritage and supports its success in canine sports and conformation.The Barbado requires thoughtful socialization and may show natural reserve with unfamiliar people. It does best with owners who value partnership, structure, and active engagement. Not suited to every household, the Barbado da Terceira rewards the right home with devotion, capability, and enduring character.

## 🧾 历史
In the 1500s, settlers brought the Barbado da Terceira to the Portuguese Azorean island of Terceira. The breed, which likely shares ancestry with the morphologically distinct Azorean Cattle Dog, is descended from medium-sized, long-haired European herding or cattle dogs. This shared ancestry is likely due to the diverse history of settlement in the Azores by the Portuguese, Spanish, French, Belgian, Flemish, English, and Dutch.In April 2004, a protocol of action for the recognition of the ¿Barbado da Terceira¿ was signed by the CPC, the Direcção Regional do Desenvolvimento Agrário (Regional Direction of Agricultural Development) and the University of the Azores. The first provisional standard of the Barbado da Terceira was presented to the CPC General Assembly members in 2005. The breed was not brought to mainland Portugal until the end of the 20th century.The Barbado's popularity as a pet, herder, and show dog is growing in Portugal and abroad, and is facilitated by the CPC, the Associação Açoreana dos Criadores dos Cães Barbados da Ilha Terceira (The Azorean Association of Barbados Dog Breeders of Terceira Island), and the Clube Português do Barbado da Terceira (CPBT).In 2018, the first registered BDTs were brought to Maryland and Michigan from Terceira Island, mainland Portugal, and the Netherlands. The first U.S.A. litter of eight was born in October 2020. The AKC recognized the BDT as Foundation Stock in January 2020. The BDT is officially recognized by the AKC-FSS Foundation Stock Service (USA), Clube Português de Canicultura (Portugal), Société Royale Saint-Hubert (Belgium), Société Centrale Canine (France), and Raad van Beheer op Kynologisch Gebied in Nederland (Dutch). The breed is also being considered for recognition by the Canadian Kennel Club and the FCI.

## 🩺 养护指南
### Health
The Barbado da Terceira is a robust and athletic breed with a muscular physique that facilitates speed and endurance. Its double coat allows it to adapt to diverse weather conditions, and its strong bones and joints make it well-suited for an active lifestyle and various sports, such as agility, herding, and flyball.Hip problems are uncommon in BDTs; however, no dog breed can claim to have perfect hips. Breeders are dedicated to continuously improving hip health through Pennhip testing of all dogs in the US. Although DNA tests show that some BDTs are carriers of MDR-1 and CDDY, there have been no reported cases of adverse effects from these conditions.

### Grooming
Bath every two months or as needed. Frequent bathing removes essential skin oils.  Should be brushed weekly or biweekly to prevent matting and remove debris. Summer trimming is unnecessary.  Shaving should be avoided, if possible, as it can permanently damage the outer coat. If desired, the hair may be scissored down to 1-1.5 inches, following the natural lines of the dog's shape. The muzzle, beard, and top of the head may be kept longer, with some strands going over the eyes, as long as the dog can still see. Hair on the muzzle should be rounded.  Nails, including dew claws, should be checked and trimmed every few weeks. Ears should be checked weekly and cleaned. Teeth should be regularly brushed.

### Exercise
The intelligent and athletic Barbado da Terceira must have daily exercise, both mental and physical, to stave off problem behaviors.BDTs are happiest when they have a cow to herd, but if these are not available, a ball, a squirrel, or a paper bag blowing in the wind will do. They willingly walk, hike, or jog with their human. Agility courses and herding courses, if available, are also great substitutes for those not living on a farm.BDTs are giddy and vocal when playing with each other. Other breeds may be intimidated by the BTD¿s earnest play and herding tendencies.A BDT rarely leaves its owner's side and doesn't typically need to learn recall due to its protective nature and instincts. Any fence built to contain a BDT should be 8 feet tall and should lack rungs to prevent climbing.

### Training
The Barbado da Terceira is a primitive breed that shares many characteristics with other herding breeds, but it stands apart due to its retention of original characteristics and instincts. These include a strong prey drive, high energy level, independence, intelligence, territorial and protective instincts, and a vocal and expressive nature.While they can make excellent companions for active individuals and families who understand and appreciate their unique traits, Barbado da Terceira are not suitable for everyone and require experienced owners who can provide them with early socialization, training, activity and mental stimulation. Early socialization includes exposure to different surfaces, noises, strangers, dogs, and public places.

### Nutrition
Dogs must always have access to clean water and high-quality food. Veterinarians should be consulted to determine whether commercially manufactured or home-prepared food is best for a dog¿s life stage.Puppies generally require puppy food four times per day up to twelve weeks old, three times per day from three to six months old, and twice daily after six months old. Puppy food must be used until the dog is fully grown.The BDT is not prone to being overweight, so free-feeding is also an option.
